What is BMI?

Definition

Body Mass Index (BMI) is a numerical value calculated from your weight and height. It categorises adults into four weight status groups: Underweight, Normal, Overweight, and Obese.

Health Screening

BMI is used by healthcare professionals as a fast, low-cost screening tool. A BMI outside the normal range can indicate increased risk of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and other conditions.

Limitations

BMI does not measure body fat directly. Athletes with high muscle mass may be classified as overweight, and elderly individuals may appear normal despite excess fat.

Who It Applies To

BMI applies to adults aged 18–65. It is not appropriate for children, pregnant women, highly muscular athletes, or those over 65, who need different assessment tools.

Personalised Advice by Category

Underweight (<18.5)
  • Increase calorie intake with nutrient-dense foods
  • Focus on protein: eggs, legumes, lean meats
  • Strength training to build healthy muscle mass
  • Consult a doctor to rule out underlying causes
Normal (18.5–24.9)
  • Maintain a balanced, varied whole-food diet
  • 150+ min moderate aerobic activity per week
  • Strength training 2–3 times per week
  • Prioritise sleep (7–9 hrs) and stress management
Overweight (25–29.9)
  • Create a modest 300–500 kcal daily deficit
  • Reduce processed foods, sugar, and refined carbs
  • Increase daily steps to 8,000–10,000/day
  • Track meals with a food diary for accountability
Obese (≥ 30)
  • Seek guidance from a registered dietitian
  • Start with low-impact exercise: walking, swimming
  • Get regular blood pressure & glucose checks
  • Set small, sustainable goals — 0.5–1 kg/week
The Formula

How BMI is Calculated

BMI was devised by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in the 1830s and later adopted by the WHO as a population-level screening tool.

BMI = weight (kg) ÷ height² (m²)

height in m = height in cm ÷ 100  ·  E.g. 70 kg ÷ (1.75)² = 22.9

The WHO classifies BMI into the four standard categories above. Some Asian-Pacific guidelines use lower thresholds due to population-specific metabolic differences.
