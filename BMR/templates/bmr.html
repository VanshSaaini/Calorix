What is BMR?

Definition

BMR is the number of calories your body needs to sustain vital functions — breathing, circulation, cell repair, and temperature regulation — while at complete rest.

Why It Matters

BMR accounts for 60–75% of your total daily calorie burn. It is the starting point for accurate fat loss, muscle gain, or maintenance targets.

BMR vs TDEE

Your TDEE = BMR × activity multiplier — it adds calories burned through movement and exercise on top of your resting baseline.

Changes Over Time

BMR drops ~2% per decade. Strength training and adequate protein intake are the most effective ways to preserve metabolic rate as you age.

Factors That Affect BMR

Age — decreases ~2% per decade after 20
Biological sex — males generally have higher BMR
Height — taller individuals need more energy
Weight — more mass means more energy at rest
Muscle mass — burns more than fat tissue at rest
Body temperature — fever raises BMR sharply
Hormones — thyroid directly regulates metabolism
Sleep quality — poor sleep suppresses metabolic rate

The Science

Mifflin–St Jeor Equation

Developed in 1990 by MD Mifflin and ST St Jeor. Endorsed by the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics as the most accurate formula for estimating resting metabolic rate in healthy adults.

♂ Men:   BMR = (10 × W) + (6.25 × H) − (5 × A) + 5
♀ Women: BMR = (10 × W) + (6.25 × H) − (5 × A) − 161

W = weight in kg  ·  H = height in cm  ·  A = age in years

Variable Reference

Variable Meaning Unit
W Body mass kg
H Body height cm
A Age years
+5 Male constant kcal/day
−161 Female constant kcal/day
Accurate within ±10% for most healthy adults — more reliable than the original Harris–Benedict (1919) formula.
